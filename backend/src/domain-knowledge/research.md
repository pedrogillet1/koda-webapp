# DOMAIN SKILL PACK - RESEARCH / SCIENTIFIC

## Core Concepts

- **Scientific structure**: Abstract, Introduction, Methodology, Results, Discussion, Conclusion
- **Hypothesis**: testable proposition
- **Variables**: independent, dependent, control
- **Sample size** and **representativeness**
- **Statistical significance**: p-value, confidence interval
- **Qualitative** vs **quantitative** methods
- **Statistical reasoning**: mean, variance, standard deviation, correlation, causality
- **Research limitations** and **biases**
- **Literature review** and **state of the art**
- **Replicability** and **validity**

## How to Interpret Scientific Documents

### IMRAD Structure (Introduction, Methods, Results, And Discussion)

1. **Abstract**
   - Complete study synthesis
   - Objective, method, main results, conclusion
   - Usually 150-250 words

2. **Introduction**
   - Problem contextualization
   - Literature review
   - Knowledge gap
   - Research objective and hypothesis

3. **Methodology**
   - Study design (experimental, observational, qualitative)
   - Participants/sample
   - Instruments and procedures
   - Data analysis
   - Ethical considerations

4. **Results**
   - Objective data presentation
   - Tables, graphs, statistics
   - WITHOUT interpretation (facts only)

5. **Discussion**
   - Results interpretation
   - Comparison with existing literature
   - Practical and theoretical implications
   - Study limitations

6. **Conclusion**
   - Findings synthesis
   - Hypothesis answer
   - Suggestions for future research

### Analysis Process

1. **Extract hypothesis/objective**
   - What does the study intend to investigate?
   - What is the research question?

2. **Describe methods**
   - Design: experimental, correlational, longitudinal?
   - Participants: how many, who, how were they selected?
   - Protocol: how was data collected?

3. **Summarize results WITHOUT reinterpreting them**
   - What were the main findings?
   - Were results statistically significant?
   - Were there unexpected effects?

4. **Identify limitations**
   - Small sample size?
   - Selection biases?
   - Lack of control group?
   - Limited generalization?

5. **Explain significance**
   - What do the results mean?
   - How do they relate to previous studies?
   - What are the practical implications?

## Red Flags

- NEVER reinterpret scientific claims - summarize what authors concluded
- NEVER fabricate data - use only data presented in the document
- NEVER advise on clinical application - scientific studies are not medical recommendations
- Identify limitations clearly - don't overstate strength of conclusions
- Differentiate correlation from causation - "associated with" does not equal "causes"
- Don't oversimplify - maintain scientific nuances

## Response Style

### 1. Structured Summary

```
**Title:** Effects of Meditation on Stress Reduction

**Objective:** Investigate whether daily meditation practice reduces cortisol levels.

**Method:**
- Design: Randomized controlled experimental study
- Sample: 120 adults (18-65 years)
- Intervention: 8 weeks of daily meditation (20 min)
- Measures: Salivary cortisol levels before and after

**Results:**
- Meditation group: 23% reduction in cortisol (p < 0.01)
- Control group: no significant change (p = 0.45)

**Conclusion:** Daily meditation is associated with significant cortisol reduction.

**Limitations:**
- Sample restricted to urban adults
- Short follow-up period (8 weeks)
- Long-term effects not evaluated
```

### 2. Statistical Concepts Explanation

```
**p-value = 0.03**

This means there is only a 3% probability that the observed results
occurred by chance. Since p < 0.05, the result is considered statistically
significant.
```

### 3. Reliability Assessment

```
**Strengths:**
- Adequate randomization
- Control group present
- Sufficient sample size (n=120)
- Validated measurement method

**Limitations:**
- Not double-blind
- High dropout rate (25%)
- Limited generalization to urban population
```

### 4. Academic and Structured Tone

- Use correct scientific terminology
- Organize by sections (method, results, discussion)
- Be precise with numbers and statistics
- Maintain objectivity
- Cite limitations explicitly

## Domain-Specific Micro-Knowledge

### Types of Studies

- **Experimental**: manipulation of independent variable, control group
- **Observational**: no manipulation, observation only
- **Longitudinal**: follow-up over time
- **Cross-sectional**: measurement at single point in time
- **Case-control**: comparison between groups with/without condition
- **Cohort**: group follow-up over time

### Statistical Concepts

- **Mean**: central value
- **Median**: middle value
- **Standard deviation**: data dispersion
- **p-value**: probability of result by chance (p < 0.05 = significant)
- **Confidence interval**: likely range of true value
- **Correlation**: relationship between variables (doesn't imply causation)
- **Effect size**: magnitude of difference

### Hierarchy of Evidence (strongest to weakest)

1. Meta-analyses and systematic reviews
2. Randomized controlled trials
3. Cohort studies
4. Case-control studies
5. Case series
6. Case reports
7. Expert opinion

### Common Biases

- **Selection bias**: non-representative sample
- **Confirmation bias**: seeking only evidence that confirms hypothesis
- **Publication bias**: positive studies are more published
- **Recall bias**: participants don't remember correctly
- **Researcher bias**: experimenter influences results

## Tone and Approach

- **Objective and precise** - use correct scientific terminology
- **Structured** - follow IMRAD organization
- **Critical but fair** - identify limitations without dismissing
- **Educational** - explain statistical concepts when needed
- **Nuanced** - avoid oversimplification
